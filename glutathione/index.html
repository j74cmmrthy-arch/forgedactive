GLUTATHIONE

GSH · Gamma-Glutamylcysteinylglycine

Often called the body's "master antioxidant," glutathione is a tripeptide (three-amino-acid molecule) produced naturally in every cell. It neutralizes free radicals (unstable molecules that damage cells), supports detoxification in the liver, and plays a central role in immune function. Levels decline with age, chronic stress, and illness.

// How It Works

Mechanism of Action

Antioxidant Defense

Neutralizing Cell Damage

Glutathione works by donating an electron to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS) — unstable molecules, also called free radicals, that damage DNA, proteins, and cell membranes when left unchecked. After donating its electron, glutathione can be recycled back to its active form by the enzyme glutathione reductase, making it a renewable defense system rather than a one-time antioxidant.

Liver Detoxification

Phase II Detox Support

In the liver, glutathione is essential to Phase II detoxification — the process that binds toxic compounds (environmental pollutants, medications, metabolic waste) to water-soluble molecules so they can be safely excreted. Low glutathione levels slow this process, allowing toxins to accumulate. This is particularly relevant during GLP protocols where metabolic rate and liver activity both increase significantly.

// Published Research

What the Science Shows

Oxidative Stress and Aging

Glutathione levels decline significantly with age and are consistently lower in people with chronic disease, metabolic dysfunction, and inflammatory conditions. Supplementation has been shown to increase plasma glutathione levels and reduce markers of oxidative stress (measurable signs of cellular damage from free radicals) in multiple human studies.

Authors: Richie JP Jr, Leutzinger Y, Parthasarathy S, et al.
Title: Methionine restriction increases blood glutathione and longevity in F344 rats
Journal: FASEB Journal
Year: 1994  |  PMID: 7926396
DOI: 10.1096/fasebj.8.15.7926396

IV and Liposomal Bioavailability

Oral glutathione has historically poor bioavailability (the body breaks much of it down before it reaches the bloodstream). Liposomal formulations (where the glutathione is encased in a fat-based shell that survives digestion) and intravenous (IV) delivery have shown significantly better absorption and measurable increases in cellular glutathione levels.

Authors: Sinha R, Sinha I, Calcagnotto A, et al.
Title: Oral supplementation with liposomal glutathione elevates body stores of glutathione and markers of immune function
Journal: European Journal of Clinical Nutrition
Year: 2018  |  PMID: 28853742
DOI: 10.1038/ejcn.2017.132

// Common Questions

Glutathione FAQ

Standard oral glutathione is largely broken down in the digestive tract before reaching the bloodstream. The molecule does not survive digestion well in its complete form. Liposomal formulations encapsulate it in a fat shell that protects it through the gut, significantly improving how much actually reaches your cells. Alternatively, supplementing with NAC (N-acetyl cysteine) gives the body the raw material to produce glutathione itself.
NAC (N-acetyl cysteine) is a precursor — your body converts it into cysteine, which is then used to synthesize glutathione. Taking NAC is an indirect way to raise glutathione levels. Direct glutathione supplementation (liposomal or IV) delivers the finished molecule. Both approaches are used in wellness protocols; some people use both simultaneously.
Glutathione has a strong safety profile across multiple forms and delivery methods. It is produced naturally in the body and is not foreign to human physiology. IV administration carries the standard risks of any IV procedure and should be done by a qualified provider. As with any supplement or compound, consult your healthcare provider before starting.
