MOTS-C

Mitochondrial Open Reading Frame · 16S rRNA

MOTS-c is a mitochondrial-derived peptide (a small protein produced by the DNA inside mitochondria — the energy-producing structures in your cells). It regulates metabolic flexibility, meaning the body's ability to switch efficiently between burning fat and carbohydrates depending on activity level and energy availability. It has been called a "mitochondrial hormone" and is considered one of the most exciting emerging compounds in longevity and metabolic research.

// How It Works

Mechanism of Action

AMPK Activation

The Energy Sensor Pathway

MOTS-c activates AMPK (AMP-activated protein kinase) — often described as the cell's master energy sensor. When AMPK is activated, cells increase glucose uptake, enhance fat oxidation (burning of fat for fuel), and improve mitochondrial biogenesis (the creation of new mitochondria). This is the same pathway activated by exercise, caloric restriction, and metformin (a common diabetes medication), which is why MOTS-c is sometimes called an "exercise mimetic."

Folate Cycle Regulation

Metabolic Flexibility

MOTS-c also regulates the folate-methionine cycle — a metabolic pathway involved in how cells process one-carbon units for DNA synthesis, methylation (a chemical process that controls gene expression), and amino acid metabolism. Disruption of this cycle is associated with metabolic disease and aging. MOTS-c's regulation of this pathway contributes to its broad effects on cellular metabolism and longevity-related processes.

// Published Research

What the Science Shows

Discovery and Metabolic Effects

The original discovery paper identified MOTS-c as a regulator of insulin sensitivity and metabolic homeostasis (maintaining stable metabolic balance). In mouse models, MOTS-c administration improved insulin sensitivity, reduced obesity, and increased exercise capacity — effects that were comparable to regular aerobic exercise training.

Authors: Lee C, Zeng J, Drew BG, et al.
Title: The Mitochondrial-Derived Peptide MOTS-c Promotes Metabolic Homeostasis and Reduces Obesity and Insulin Resistance
Journal: Cell Metabolism
Year: 2015  |  PMID: 25738459
DOI: 10.1016/j.cmet.2015.02.003

Exercise Performance and Muscle

Research has shown MOTS-c levels rise in skeletal muscle (the muscle attached to your skeleton that you control voluntarily) during exercise and decline with aging. Exogenous (externally administered) MOTS-c improved physical performance and muscle endurance in aged mice, suggesting a role in combating age-related decline in exercise capacity and muscle function.

Authors: Reynolds JC, Bhatt DL, Kim SJ, et al.
Title: MOTS-c is an exercise-induced mitochondrial encoded regulator of age-dependent physical decline and muscle homeostasis
Journal: Nature Communications
Year: 2021  |  PMID: 34385433
DOI: 10.1038/s41467-021-24509-5

// What to Expect

General Timeline

Based on community experience and emerging research. MOTS-c has a shorter published human track record than other compounds on this site — approach with appropriate patience and monitoring.

1–2

Weeks 1–2

Improved energy during workouts is commonly the first reported change — a noticeable increase in endurance and reduced perceived effort at the same intensity. Some users report better blood sugar stability throughout the day.

3–6

Weeks 3–6

Recovery between training sessions improves. Body composition may begin shifting — less fat accumulation and better muscle retention, particularly in those combining MOTS-c with regular training. Metabolic flexibility improvements (less energy crashes, more stable energy) become more consistent.

8–12

Weeks 8–12

Cumulative effects on body composition, endurance, and metabolic stability are most apparent with consistent use and continued training. Community users often describe this as a "baseline lift" — feeling stronger and more metabolically capable at rest and during exertion.

// Experience and Protocol

How It Is Used

Personal insights coming soon. In the meantime, here is how the broader wellness community approaches MOTS-c.

Community Protocol

Common Approach

  • Dose: 5–10mg subcutaneous (subQ) injection, 3–5 times per week
  • Timing: Before exercise or in the morning to align with natural AMPK activation from activity
  • Injection site: Abdomen or dorsogluteal (upper outer buttock), rotating sites
  • Cycle: 8–12 weeks on, 4–6 weeks off is the most common approach in the community
  • Stack: Often used alongside NAD+ precursors (NMN or NR), which work synergistically with MOTS-c's mitochondrial activation pathway
  • Some users prefer daily lower-dose protocols (5mg daily) over higher-dose less-frequent ones
Why Use It Alongside GLP Protocols

The Complement Case

  • GLP protocols significantly reduce caloric intake — MOTS-c supports the metabolic machinery needed to efficiently use the reduced energy available
  • AMPK activation from MOTS-c promotes fat oxidation (burning fat for fuel), which complements the fat loss goals of GLP protocols
  • Muscle preservation during rapid weight loss is a known challenge — MOTS-c's role in muscle homeostasis (maintaining stable muscle tissue) may help offset some of that loss
  • The exercise mimetic properties are valuable when reduced appetite also reduces energy available for training

// Common Questions

MOTS-c FAQ

An exercise mimetic is a compound that activates some of the same cellular pathways that physical exercise does — in this case, AMPK activation, increased fat oxidation, and improved mitochondrial function. This does not mean MOTS-c replaces exercise — it cannot replicate the cardiovascular, structural, and hormonal benefits of actual training. But it can support the metabolic environment that makes exercise more effective and recovery faster.
MOTS-c is a research compound and is not FDA approved for any indication. Most published data comes from preclinical models (mice and cell studies). Early human research is underway but long-term safety data in humans is still limited. It is used in wellness contexts under provider supervision.
MOTS-c levels naturally decline with age, and this decline correlates with reduced metabolic flexibility, increased insulin resistance, and diminished physical capacity — all hallmarks of metabolic aging. Research interest in MOTS-c as a longevity compound stems from the possibility that restoring age-depleted levels could delay or partially reverse some of these changes. It is one of several mitochondrial-derived peptides (including humanin and SHLP peptides) being studied in this context.
