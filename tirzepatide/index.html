TIRZEPATIDE

Dual Agonist · LY3298176

A compound that activates two hormone receptors — GIP and GLP-1 — at the same time. This dual action makes it one of the most effective metabolic compounds currently available, with extensive clinical trial data behind it.

// How It Works

Mechanism of Action

GLP-1 Receptor

Appetite and Blood Sugar Control

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a hormone your gut naturally releases after eating. Activating its receptor slows digestion, signals your brain that you are full, and prompts the pancreas to release insulin only when blood sugar is elevated. The practical effect is a significant reduction in appetite and improved blood sugar stability — including the quieting of "food noise," the constant mental background pull toward eating that many people do not realize is there until it stops.

GIP Receptor

Insulin Sensitivity

GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide) is another gut hormone that works alongside GLP-1. Tirzepatide's dual action on GIP receptors improves how well fat tissue responds to insulin, which helps the body process energy more efficiently. This is also what differentiates tirzepatide from semaglutide (Ozempic/Wegovy), which only targets GLP-1.

// Published Research

What the Science Says

SURMOUNT-1 — Phase 3 Obesity Trial

Participants on tirzepatide 15mg lost an average of 20.9% of their body weight over 72 weeks. This was the highest weight loss ever recorded in a non-surgical medication trial at the time. More than half of participants at the highest dose lost at least 20% of their body weight. The placebo group lost 3.1%.

Authors: Jastreboff AM, Aronne LJ, Ahmad NN, et al.
Title: Tirzepatide Once Weekly for the Treatment of Obesity
Journal: New England Journal of Medicine
Year: 2022  |  PMID: 35658024
DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa2206038

SURPASS-2 — Head-to-Head vs. Semaglutide

In the only direct Phase 3 comparison between tirzepatide and semaglutide 1mg, tirzepatide at all doses produced significantly greater reductions in both blood sugar (HbA1c) and body weight. Tirzepatide 15mg reduced HbA1c by 2.46% versus 1.86% for semaglutide, and produced nearly double the weight loss (11.2kg vs 5.7kg).

Authors: Frías JP, Davies MJ, Rosenstock J, et al.
Title: Tirzepatide versus Semaglutide Once Weekly in Patients with Type 2 Diabetes
Journal: New England Journal of Medicine
Year: 2021  |  PMID: 34170647
DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa2107519

// What to Expect

Week by Week

Based on family experience and community-reported progression. Individual results vary based on starting dose, titration pace, age, and overall health profile.

1–2

Weeks 1–2

Appetite suppression begins, often from the first injection. Many people are surprised by how quickly hunger signals change. Nausea is most likely in these first two weeks — eating smaller, more frequent meals and avoiding high-fat or greasy foods helps significantly.

2–4

Weeks 2–4

Weight loss typically begins. A steady pace of 0.5–1 pound per week is common and healthy. Nausea often starts to ease as the body adjusts. Energy may feel lower as caloric intake drops — this is normal and typically stabilizes.

4–8

Weeks 4–8

The "food noise" reduction — the constant mental background pull toward eating — becomes clearly noticeable for most people. This is often cited as the most unexpected and meaningful quality-of-life change. Body composition changes begin to be visible.

8–12

Weeks 8–12

Consistent weight loss continues. Blood sugar stability, energy levels, and overall sense of wellbeing typically improve. Dose increases may happen in this window if tolerated and warranted — always with adequate time between increases.

12+

Week 12 and Beyond

Sustained results with appropriate lifestyle support. Plateaus are normal and are not a sign the compound stopped working — they are the body adapting. Re-evaluating nutrition quality and activity level is the first step before considering dose changes.

// Experience and Protocol

How It Was Used

This section covers a close family member's firsthand experience alongside the general protocol approach that worked well. These are starting points for your own conversation with a healthcare provider, not instructions to follow exactly.

Personal Experience

Family Member — 74 Years Old

My mother followed a careful, slow titration (gradual dose increase) schedule: 2.5mg weekly for 6 weeks, then 5mg weekly for 6 weeks, then 7.5mg split across the week. She is 74 and on blood pressure medication, so moving slowly was important for safety.


She started losing weight within the first two weeks and consistently lost around 0.75 pounds per week throughout the protocol — a safe, steady pace for her age and health profile. All injections were subcutaneous (subQ), meaning into the fatty layer just under the skin, not into muscle.


Her experience is a strong reminder that you do not need to rush dose increases. The compound was working at every level, and moving carefully kept her comfortable and consistent.

Protocol Guidance

What Worked and What to Consider

Standard titration begins at 2.5mg weekly. Most protocols increase by 2.5mg every 4 weeks, but there is no obligation to follow that pace. If you are tolerating the current dose well and seeing results, staying at that dose longer is a completely valid choice.


Key priorities throughout any tirzepatide protocol:

  • Prioritize protein at every meal — aim for 0.8g per pound of body weight daily
  • Add or maintain resistance training (weight lifting) to protect muscle
  • Stay well hydrated and include electrolytes if you feel fatigued
  • Increase fiber intake to support digestion, which slows significantly on GLP compounds
  • Judge dose increases on hunger signals and how you feel, not the scale alone

The compound is doing its job even at lower doses. There is no prize for being on the highest dose fastest.

// Common Questions

Tirzepatide FAQ

Tirzepatide targets two hormone receptors: GIP and GLP-1. Retatrutide targets three: GIP, GLP-1, and glucagon. The addition of glucagon receptor activity in retatrutide increases how many calories your body burns at rest, which tirzepatide does not do. Phase 2 trial data shows retatrutide producing greater weight loss, but tirzepatide has full FDA approval and a much larger base of long-term safety data. Tirzepatide is the more established option; retatrutide is more experimental.
Semaglutide only activates the GLP-1 receptor. Tirzepatide activates both GLP-1 and GIP receptors, which amplifies the metabolic effects. Direct trial data (SURPASS-2) showed tirzepatide producing significantly greater weight loss and blood sugar improvement than semaglutide 1mg. The GIP component may also reduce the intensity of GLP-1-related side effects like nausea.
The most common side effects are gastrointestinal (GI) — meaning digestive — and include nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, and constipation. These are most pronounced during dose increases and typically improve within 2 to 4 weeks of staying at a stable dose. Slowing your titration (dose increase schedule) is the most effective way to manage them.
Not required, but strongly worth it. Because your appetite drops significantly, the quality of what you eat matters more than it did before. Prioritize protein-dense foods, add fiber to support digestion, and avoid high-fat greasy meals which tend to worsen nausea. Staying hydrated is important and frequently underestimated.
Most people regain some weight after stopping because the appetite-regulating effect is tied to the compound being present. This is why building lasting habits during the protocol matters so much. A maintenance dose (a lower ongoing dose to preserve results) is often a better long-term strategy than stopping entirely.
