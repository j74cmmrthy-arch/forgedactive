KPV

Lys-Pro-Val · Alpha-MSH Fragment

KPV is a small three-amino-acid peptide (Lysine-Proline-Valine) derived from alpha-melanocyte stimulating hormone (alpha-MSH), a naturally occurring hormone involved in regulating inflammation. It has demonstrated anti-inflammatory, antimicrobial, and wound-healing properties across multiple research models, with particular promise for gut and skin conditions.

// How It Works

Mechanism of Action

MC1R Receptor

Anti-Inflammatory Signaling

KPV works primarily by binding to melanocortin receptors — cell-surface proteins involved in regulating the body's inflammatory response. When these receptors are activated, they suppress the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines (chemical signals that promote inflammation) and dampen the inflammatory cascade. The result is reduced systemic and localized inflammation without broadly suppressing the immune system.

Gut and Mucosal Activity

Gut Lining Support

Research has shown KPV has direct protective effects on intestinal epithelial cells — the cells that line the gut wall. It reduces inflammatory signaling in gut tissue and supports the integrity of the mucosal barrier (the protective lining that prevents gut contents from leaking into surrounding tissue). This makes it relevant for people dealing with gut inflammation or digestive irritation.

// Published Research

What the Science Shows

Inflammatory Bowel Disease Models

In preclinical models of inflammatory bowel disease (IBD — conditions where the digestive tract becomes chronically inflamed, such as Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis), KPV significantly reduced intestinal inflammation and improved mucosal healing. Oral and intracolonic administration both showed effects, suggesting the peptide survives partial digestion and acts locally in the gut.

Title: PepT1-mediated tripeptide KPV uptake reduces intestinal inflammation

Skin Inflammation and Wound Healing

KPV has shown anti-inflammatory activity in skin models, reducing redness, swelling, and cytokine expression associated with inflammatory skin conditions. Its small size allows it to penetrate skin tissue effectively, which is relevant for topical applications and conditions like eczema or contact dermatitis (skin inflammation triggered by irritants or allergens).

// Experience and Protocol

How It Was Used

Personal Experience

My Protocol

I used 0.2mg (200mcg) daily via subcutaneous (subQ) injection — into the fatty layer under the skin, not into muscle. I rotated between the left and right love handles (the soft tissue at the sides of the lower abdomen) each day to avoid repeated irritation at any single site.


Results were noticeable within one week. My face looked less puffy, redness and flushing that I normally experienced calmed significantly, and all of my eczema spots settled down. Areas that would previously flare red with minor irritation stopped reacting as intensely.


I ran two months on KPV, then took a one-month break. This cycling approach is common and gives the body a reset period before the next cycle.

Community Protocol

General Approach

Across the wellness and biohacking community, KPV is typically used at doses ranging from 0.2mg to 0.5mg daily, either via subcutaneous injection or oral capsule. Oral use takes advantage of KPV's stability in the gut and its ability to act directly on intestinal tissue — making it a reasonable option for those primarily targeting gut inflammation.


For skin and systemic inflammation, subcutaneous injection delivers the compound into the bloodstream more directly. Injection site rotation is important regardless of location chosen.


Most community protocols use a cycle approach — 6 to 8 weeks on, followed by a break of 2 to 4 weeks — though individual approaches vary based on goals and response.
