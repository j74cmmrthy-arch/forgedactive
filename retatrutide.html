RETATRUTIDE

Triple Agonist · LY3437943

A next-generation compound that activates three hormone receptors simultaneously — GIP, GLP-1, and glucagon. Phase 2 clinical trial data showed over 24% average body weight reduction at 48 weeks, the highest ever recorded in a medication trial. Phase 3 trials are currently underway.

// How It Works

Triple Action Explained

GLP-1

Appetite and Insulin

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) slows digestion, reduces appetite, and triggers insulin release in response to food. This is the same mechanism shared with tirzepatide and semaglutide — it forms the foundation of retatrutide's effect profile.

GIP

Insulin Sensitivity

GIP (glucose-dependent insulinotropic peptide) improves how well fat tissue responds to insulin and supports the body's ability to shift efficiently between burning fat and carbohydrates for energy.

Glucagon

The Third Axis

Glucagon is a hormone that normally raises blood sugar and promotes fat breakdown. On its own, it would cause blood sugar to spike — but when combined with GLP-1 agonism, that effect is suppressed while the fat-burning and calorie-burning activity remains. This is what gives retatrutide its thermogenic (heat and energy-producing) edge over dual agonists.

Combined Effect

Why Three Receptors

GLP-1 reduces what you eat. GIP improves how your body uses insulin. Glucagon increases how many calories your body burns at rest. Together they address metabolic dysfunction from three angles at once, producing outcomes that exceed what any single or dual agonist can achieve.

// Published Research

What the Data Shows

Phase 2 Trial — Primary Outcomes

Participants receiving retatrutide 12mg weekly lost an average of 24.2% of their body weight at 48 weeks — the highest weight reduction ever reported in a medication trial at the time. The placebo group lost 2.1%. The results continued to improve at the highest dose, suggesting the maximum effective dose has not yet been identified.

Authors: Jastreboff AM, Kaplan LM, Frías JP, et al.
Title: Triple-Hormone-Receptor Agonist Retatrutide for Obesity — A Phase 2 Trial
Journal: New England Journal of Medicine
Year: 2023  |  PMID: 37366315
DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa2301972

Liver Fat Reduction

Retatrutide produced significant reductions in liver fat, relevant for the estimated 25 to 30% of adults with non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD — a condition where excess fat builds up in the liver, often without obvious symptoms). The glucagon receptor component promotes fat burning directly in the liver, which drives much of this benefit.

Authors: Jastreboff AM, Kaplan LM, Frías JP, et al.
Title: Triple-Hormone-Receptor Agonist Retatrutide for Obesity — A Phase 2 Trial
Journal: New England Journal of Medicine
Year: 2023  |  PMID: 37366315
DOI: 10.1056/NEJMoa2301972

Cardiometabolic Markers

Phase 2 participants showed improvements across fasting glucose (blood sugar measured after not eating), HbA1c (a 3-month average of blood sugar levels), triglycerides (fats in the blood), and blood pressure. Mild increases in resting heart rate of about 4 to 5 beats per minute were observed at higher doses — a known effect of glucagon receptor activity that warrants monitoring.

// Experience and Protocol

How It Was Used

Personal experience with dosing protocol, injection approach, and key lessons learned — alongside what a more conservative approach would look like in hindsight. All injections were subcutaneous (subQ), meaning into the fatty layer just under the skin, not into muscle.

My Protocol

What I Actually Did

I injected into dedicated spots around my stomach, rotating the site each time to avoid tissue buildup. My dose progression was:

  • 1mg weekly for 2 weeks
  • 2mg weekly for 6 weeks
  • 4mg weekly for 4 weeks
  • 5mg weekly for 2 weeks
  • 6mg weekly for 3 weeks
  • 7mg+ split every 4 days (to manage side effects)

I started losing weight in the first week. The fastest rate was 2 pounds per week, which I eventually had to moderate because the loss was too rapid. I maintained around 0.6 pounds per week once I stabilized. I plan to taper to a maintenance dose to help sustain the habits built during the protocol.

What I Would Recommend

A More Conservative Approach

In hindsight, I titrated (gradually increased dose) too fast. Retatrutide reaches its maximum concentration in the body about 4 weeks after a dose change — which means you cannot fully judge a dose's effect until after that point. Here is what I would suggest:

  • Start at 1mg weekly
  • Stay for at least 6 weeks before considering an increase
  • Increase by 0.5mg at a time, not larger jumps
  • Judge readiness to increase based on hunger signals, not the scale alone
  • A good dose is where you can still eat on a regular schedule — not one where you avoid food altogether
  • Split dosing (dividing the weekly amount into two smaller injections across the week) can significantly reduce side effects like allodynia and constipation

Prioritizing protein, lifting weights, drinking enough water with electrolytes, and getting adequate fiber are not optional additions — they are core to getting the most out of this protocol and protecting your body during rapid change.

Side Effects I Experienced

Allodynia and Constipation

Around months 3 and 4, I developed allodynia — a skin sensitivity where even light contact, like clothing touching certain areas, felt like the discomfort of a healing sunburn. It was not painful but was noticeable enough that I changed my approach. Splitting the weekly dose into two smaller injections every 4 days resolved it significantly.


Constipation (infrequent, difficult bowel movements) was the other notable side effect. This is common on GLP compounds because digestion slows considerably. Increasing fiber intake — vegetables, legumes, whole grains — made a meaningful difference.

Body Composition Note

DEXA Results

A DEXA scan (dual-energy X-ray scan that measures fat, muscle, and bone separately) showed I lost muscle alongside fat despite my efforts. This reinforced that resistance training (weight lifting) and high protein intake are genuinely non-negotiable during any rapid weight loss protocol, not just nice-to-haves.


Even if nothing else changes in your lifestyle, protecting muscle with protein and lifting should be the two constants throughout the entire protocol.

// Common Questions

Retatrutide FAQ

Tirzepatide activates two receptors: GIP and GLP-1. Retatrutide activates three: GIP, GLP-1, and glucagon. The glucagon component increases how many calories your body burns at rest — something tirzepatide does not do. Phase 2 data shows retatrutide producing greater weight loss, but tirzepatide is FDA approved with far more real-world safety data behind it. Retatrutide is still in Phase 3 trials.
Not as of this writing. Retatrutide is currently in Phase 3 clinical trials. Phase 3 data will establish the long-term safety profile needed for regulatory review and potential approval.
Allodynia is a condition where normally painless sensations — like clothing touching your skin — cause discomfort or a burning feeling. It has been reported by some retatrutide users, likely related to the glucagon receptor component. It is not dangerous but can be uncomfortable. Splitting the weekly dose into two smaller injections across the week is a commonly reported way to reduce it.
Heart rate is the most important additional metric to track, given the glucagon component. Mild resting heart rate increases of 4 to 5 beats per minute were documented in Phase 2. Blood pressure, cardiovascular symptoms, liver enzymes, and the standard GLP panel (lipase, amylase) should all be tracked with your provider.
Retatrutide has a long half-life (the time it takes the body to process half of the compound), which means it builds up gradually after each injection. It takes approximately 4 weeks of consistent weekly dosing at the same amount to reach steady-state — the point where blood levels have stabilized and the full effect of that dose is present. Judging a dose before that point gives you incomplete information.
